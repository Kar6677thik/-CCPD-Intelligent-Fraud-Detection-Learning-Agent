With the growth of online shopping, Credit Card Fraud (CCF) comes out as a serious menace. For this end, the 
automatic and real-time fraud detection field calls for several studies. The recent ones use many Machine 
Learning (ML) methods and techniques due to their beneficial characteristics to build a good fitting model to 
catch fraudulent transactions. The purpose of this paper is to develop a new model based on a hybrid approach 
for Credit Card Fraud Detection (CCFD). The proposed model, compared to previous studies, shows its strong 
ability to identify fraud transactions. Precisely, the robustness of our model is built by combining the strength of 
three sub-methods; the Recursive Feature Elimination (RFE) for selecting the most useful predictive features, the 
GridSearchCV for Hyper-Parameters Optimization (HPO) and the Synthetic Minority Oversampling (SMOTE) to 
overcome the imbalanced data problem. The experimentations of our model, on many real-world data sets, gives 
the best results in terms of efficiency and effectiveness.   
1.Introduction 
Globalization has amplified the use of the Internet for online shop-
ping where the credit cards are widely required. Also, the quick and big 
number of Credit Card Transactions (CCT) led to an important rise in 
fraud cases [1–3]. Therefore, it is judicious to find new techniques and 
methods able to detect these frauds. Fraud can be defined as an unjust or 
criminal deception intended to end in personal gain [4–6]. A credit card 
is a physical medium for selling goods or services without having cash in 
hand. Credit Card Fraud Detection (CCFD) is the procedure to identify 
whether a transaction is normal or abnormal. Credit Card Crime (CCC) 
[4,6] refers to the way of stealing the identity of someone (victim) and 
accomplish fraudulent transactions in its name (Fig. 1). Consequently, 
the victim can be prone to unpredicted consequences [6–8]. 
The fraudulent transactions are divided into two kinds: online fraud 
and offline fraud; the first is performed by using a stolen physical card at 
the market shop (for example) etc…, while the second is committed by 
stealing victim identities such as credit card numbers, name of the credit 
card holder, expiry date, and passwords [6–8]. 
The classification of fraudulent transactions with highly imbalanced 
classes of data becomes very challenging. Indeed, the number of normal 
transactions belonging to the normal class is, comparatively, much 
larger than the number of fraudulent transactions class which contains abnormal cases [9,10] . So, whatever the used Fraud Identification 
Model (FIM) [11,12] , we should identify, firstly, the minority-class 
samples (fraudulent transactions) apart from the majority-class sam-
ples (legitimates transactions) [6,13,14] . Fig. 2 shows an example of 
imbalanced data where the orange dots represent the fraud cases with 
much lower frequency compared to the legitimate cases represented by 
the blue dots. 
The detection of Credit Card Fraud (CCF) becomes a relevant 
research area in the last few years and several studies use Machine 
Learning (ML) and Data Mining (DM) approaches to handle this problem 
[15–19]. Indeed, in the literature we find two main approaches to 
identify and detect the fraud transactions: the supervised approach and 
the unsupervised approach [2,20 –22]. The supervised approach, a 
learning algorithm is performed to classify new transactions (fraudulent 
and non-fraudulent) based on a transactional data record [15,20] . The 
well-known algorithms, used for supervised identification and detection 
of CCF, are as follows: Bayesian Belief Networks [20], Random Forests 
[23,24] and K-Nearest Neighbors [17,25] . Support Vector Machines [16, 
17,26] , Artificial Neural Networks [17,22] and Hidden Markov Models 
[27]. The unsupervised approach, algorithms are designed to detect the 
hidden patterns in non-labeled transactional data [28]. As examples of 
the used unsupervised methods, we found SOMs (Self-Organized Maps) 
method and the K-Means method for problems associated with

Although, the deployed efforts to detect the CCF, the fight against 
this kind of cyber-crime [32] is still in the beginning due to numerous 
barriers. Indeed, previous studies have involved the utilization of 
updated data sets to investigate frauds; but because of many security 
concerns, banks and financial companies prohibit disclosure of their 
sensitive data on CCT, which limits the CCFD investigations [33]. In 
another side, the conventional metrics used to evaluate a CCFD model, 
such as accuracy and specificity, which measure the prediction cor-
rectness, are not enough to conclude about the efficiency of the model 
for identifying and detecting fraudulent transactions; so, the sensitivity 
metric, which measures the model efficiency, is needed to well study the 
performance of the given problem classification [6,27] . The F-score [27, 
34] is considered as a more realistic metric to measure the performance 
of the prediction of a CCF test, by combining both sensitivity (recall) and 
precision metrics. The F-score measures the prediction of a classification 
test and balances the use of sensitivity and precision to do so [34,35] . 
In the literature, we found many studies performed their models 
without the identification of predictive features. The consideration of all 
features for prediction may give an inadequate start for the fraud 
detection process and may not accurately reflect the cardholder 
behavior in terms of expenditure [19,35,36] . Thus, the use of all features 
without a better selection of predictive ones may not result in a better 
sensitivity of fraud detection and minimization of false alarms [6,27] . 
Briefly, a selection of predictive transactional features is very important 
to build an optimized fraud detection model [37,38] . To have a mean -
ingful selection of features for the studied model, the availability of 
various real-world databases is needed, necessary to obtain an accurate 
evaluation of performance. 
In the real-world, fraud cases (minority-class) are considered as rare events, which make their identification so difficult. Consequently, ac-
curate classification of minority-class samples in case of an imbalanced 
dataset is still a big challenge. Whereas, achieving the best performance 
in the CCF detection process, critically, depends on both the way to 
estimate the hyper-parameters of the used model and the way to select 
the predictive features. 
In this paper, we propose a hybrid model, to improve prediction 
efficiency along the process of CCFD. This hybrid model combines su-
pervised and unsupervised anomaly detection by taking advantage of 
the robustness of many ML methods and techniques. We have used the 
Hyper-Parameters Optimization (HPO) method to estimate the hyper- 
parameters of our model and the Recursive Features Elimination (RFE) 
method to select the predictive features [16]. Moreover, to overcome the 
problem of the imbalanced dataset and to increment the learning rate, 
we have used the technique called Synthetic Minority Oversampling 
Technique (SMOTE) [39]. 
The rest of this paper is structured as follows: Section 2 presents 
related works. Section 3 details our proposed model based on a hybrid 
approach. Evaluation metrics are given in Section 4. Experiments and 
discussion of the obtained results are detailed in Section 5. The last 
section concludes the paper with some perspectives. 
2.Related works 
Credit card datasets contain details of transactions such as account 
numbers, credit card and purchase types, transaction location and time, 
customer name, merchant code, transaction size, etc. These details are 
used by the CCFD research community as vectors to determine whether 
a transaction is fraudulent or legitimate and to study the outliers that 
can present a suspect case. Wei et al. [40] presented an online Fraud 
Detection System (FDS) and proved its efficiency in large volumes of 
extremely imbalanced data. Padmaja et al. [41] presented a fraud 
detection method that combines backpropagation, naïve Bayes, and 
C4.5 tree algorithms, and applied them to derived data from over-
sampling with replacement. Bolton and Hand [42] proposed two clus-
tering techniques: peer group analysis and breakpoint analysis to detect 
behavioral fraud. Tax et al. [43] studied an intuitive solution: generating 
uniformly distributed random outliers in the hypercube that encloses the 
target data to guide hyper-parameter selection, and they further 
improved the hypercube into a hyper-sphere to better fit the target data. 
Weston et al. [44] used peer group analysis on transaction datasets to 
detect outliers and abnormal transactions. Genetic algorithms combined 
with scatter search was used to minimize the number of wrongfully 
classified transactions [45]. Bahnsen et al. [46]suggested a 
cost-sensitive model that supposes a Bayes minimum risk to identify -
fraud cases. Kamaruddin and Ravi [47] proposed a one-class classifi -
cation approach to overcome the imbalanced data problem. Precisely, 
they developed a hybrid system composed of Particle Swarm Optimi -
zation and Auto Associative Neural Network and implemented in the 
Spark computational framework. 
Many other studies worked on the same goal with other techniques 
[2,48 –52]. Ramakalyani and Umadevi [49] applied genetic program -
ming to detect fraudulent card transactions. Bentley et al. [50] devel -
oped a fuzzy Darwinian detection model based on genetic programming 
to generate fuzzy logic rules. Additionally, Ganji and Mannem [51] 
suggested a data stream outlier detection algorithm based on reverse 
K-nearest neighbors to identify fraud. Bhattacharyya et al. [52] studied 
the comparison between logistic regression, SVM, and random forest; 
they note that random forest is well outperformed compared to the two 
other methods. Hormozi et al. [48] developed a CCFD system by 
executing the Negative Selection Algorithm (belong to the artificial 
immune system algorithms family) by using the Hadoop and Map 
Reduce paradigm. Carcillo et al. [2] proposed an active learning 
approach to label fraudulent transactions. 
Further scientific researches developed many other ML algorithms 
based on a neural network; Ogwueleka [53] used an artificial neural

data. They detected frauds by exploiting only legitimate transactions 
through their model definition process, which is less influenced by the 
variation of data. In a second work, Saia and Carta [64] used the Linear 
Dependence Based (LDB) model and compare its performance with 
random forests as one of the well-known state-of-the-art models. They 
validated their work by performing the model on two real-world data 
sets having a strong imbalanced data distribution. In [65], they took 
benefits of the analysis of an evaluation criterion, in terms of domain 
frequency, of the spectral pattern of the data. Their method allows 
obtaining a more stable model to represent information and reduce 
problems of imbalance and heterogeneity of data. 
Salazar et al. [36] discussed some theoretical and practical solutions 
after stirring up the main issues related to the problem of Automatic

widely used in the financial business. Zareapoor et al. [14] integrated a sampling technique with a set of

Precisely, through an empirical experiment, their used technique shows 
more appropriate performance measures for mining imbalanced data-
sets. In [67], Zareapoor et al. developed a balancing strategy to over-
come the well-known issues of classification and collection in the CCFD 
field. They created a contrast vector by customer based on its historical 
behaviors and perform a supervised learning model to classify cus-
tomers. The model experimented on a real credit card dataset provided 
by FICO, shows significant performance compared to the other 
state-of-the-art classifiers. In other work, Zareapoor et al. [38] presented 
a hybrid model, to handle datasets with a large number of classes, which 
substitute linear kernel for nonlinear ones without losing accuracy. It is 
performed on a real-world dataset with 20,000 to 65,000 classes, and it 
gives significant gains compared to several state-of-the-art approaches. 
Furthermore, Mahmoudi and Duman[15] proposed, for the first 
time, a modified Fisher discriminant function by using simple linear 
discriminant analysis for CCFD, and customized it to be more sensitive to 
the False Negatives (FN) errors. Rtayli and Enneya introduced a CCFD 
method based on a random forest classifier, as feature-filter, combined 
with isolation forest technique, to split fraudulent transactions from the 
legitimate ones [68]. Kültür and Çaglayan[20] used a model per card 
account that combines six types of models: Decision Trees, Random 
Forests, Bayesian Networks, Naive Bayes, SVM, and k-nearest neighbors. 
Sohony et al. [23] combined two methods, random forest, and 
feed-forward neural network. 
Artificial Neural Networks (ANN) for CCFD were discussed in several 
works [24,69,70]. Among ANN types, there are deep and shallow 
learning; the first has a complex structure with more than one hidden 
layer and more nodes, in each one, compared to the shallow model. Roy 
et al. [69], and Jurgovsky et al. [24] adopted recurrent neural networks, 
which use a sequence of transactions as an input of their model. Also, 
Gupta et al. [70] compared various machine learning models based on a 
deep feed-forward neural network. Finally, Makki et al. [6] conducted 
an experimental study with solutions that tackle the imbalance classi -
fication problem. They explored these adopted methods along with the 
machine learning algorithms used for fraud detection and identified 
their weaknesses. 
3.Our proposed approach 
Through this section, we present the principal components of our 
proposed hybrid model based on the three techniques/methods: the 
Support Vector Machine-Recursive Feature Elimination (SVM-RFE) 
technique to extract best predictive features, the Synthetic Minority 
Oversampling Technique (SMOTE) technique to transform an imbal -
anced dataset to a balanced one and the GridSearchCV method [71], as a 
Hyper Parameter Optimization (HPO) solution, used by a Random 
Forest Classifier (RFC), to detect Credit Card Frauds (CCFs). The pro-
posed model is denoted as RFC (HPO,RFE) .

3.2. The imbalanced data classification problem 
The imbalanced data classification still a major problem in the pro-
cess of fraud detection. Indeed, with an imbalanced dataset, we find a far 
few training instances of one class-variable than the other class-variable. 
Correspondingly, the first is known as the minority set and the second is 
known as the majority set [9]. While performing an imbalanced dataset 
to detect fraudulent transactions, most classification models rank the 
majority class with a very high accuracy rate and the minority class with 
a much lower one. This means that the proposed classification model has 
a bad performance to diagnosis the minority class samples. 
To overcome this class imbalance problem, we use the SMOTE 
technique to have a more accurate experiment. The SMOTE technique is 
an oversampling approach that uses a technique of producing arbitrary 
examples rather than simply oversampling through duplication or 
replacement. Furthermore, the SMOTE technique is able to increment 
the learning process of a Fraud Detection Algorithm (FDA) [39,75] . 
According to Kumari and Mishra [39], SMOTE technique ’s algorithm 
can be written as follows: 
Step 1: Putting the minority class (fraudulent transactions) set A, for 
each x ∃A, the k-nearest neighbors of x are acquired by computing the 
Euclidean distance between x and every other sample in set A. 
Step2 : The sampling rate N is set corresponding to the imbalanced 
amount. For each x ∃A, N examples are randomly chosen from its k- 
nearest neighbors, and they build the set A1. 
Step3: For each example xk ∃A1(k1, 2, 3…N), the following

essential [16,25] .

SVM-RFE (RFE based on Support Vector Machine) version, which 
showed to be one of the best feature selection methods; it classifies 
features (genes) by performing a vector machine classification model.

The assessment process of our RFC (HPO,RFE ) is based on the k-fold 
cross-validation of the time series method [92,93] by adopting k10 to 
validate our approach. The available data is split into k series, keeping 
the chronology of transactions. Then, the predictive model is estimated k 
times, each time using a training data set containing k-1 of the 
sub-samples. The rest of the sub-sample is maintained as validation data, 
serving to estimate the out-of-sample performance of the fitted model. 
This results in a less biased and more representative estimate of model 
efficiency [39]. 
The problem of data imbalance (described in Section 3.2) was 
encountered during the experiments. We preferred to evaluate the 
effectiveness of our approach by taking into consideration data pre- 
processing (e.g., selection features, under-sampling or over-sampling 
balancing process), which is a crucial step in improving the perfor -
mance of a classifier, especially in the area of fraud detection [7,39,47, 
48,51,76] .

6.Conclusion 
The identification of fraudulent transactions is still very difficult and 
challenging. Through this work, our purpose was to improve the pre-
diction efficiency, during the CCFD process, by exploiting the strength of 
many Machine Learning methods. Our model based on a hybrid 
approach combines three methods: The Recursive Features Elimination 
(RFE) method to reduce the number of features, the Hyper-Parameters 
Optimization (HPO) method to estimate the optimized hyper -
parameters to our RFC based model and the Synthetic Minority Over-
sampling Technique (SMOTE) to overcome the problem of imbalanced 
data. To validate our results, we performed our model on three big 
datasets, used as referenced datasets by the Machine Learning commu -
nity, and it shows its capacity to achieve a high accuracy performance 
during the CCFD process. Moreover, in terms of comparison with recent 
works, our model ensures a very good performance whatever the used 
datasets. As a next work, we plan to study an extended model on more 
complex real-world datasets characterized by a high degree of concept- drift and highly imbalanced data [3,102,103] and to develop an adap-
tive CCFD System (ACCFDS).
